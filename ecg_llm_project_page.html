ECG is All You Need — Cross-Modal Cardiac Inference with Large Language Models

Can a general-purpose LLM infer what an echocardiogram would show, from a photo of a 12-lead ECG alone?

Abstract

An ECG measures the heart's electrical activity; an echocardiogram measures its structure and function. They are conventionally treated as separate worlds. This study asked a provocative question: can a modern large language model, given only an image of a 12-lead ECG, generate a plausible echocardiographic assessment — performing cross-modal inference with no training of its own?

In a retrospective comparison of 311 paired ECG–echocardiogram cases, three leading LLMs were prompted to produce complete echo reports from ECG images alone, zero-shot. Outputs were compared against cardiologist-authored reports across ejection fraction, diastolic dysfunction, valve regurgitation and wall-motion. The best model reached a Pearson correlation of 0.394 (p < 0.001) for ejection fraction, with roughly two-thirds of estimates within ±5% of the true value. Performance is not yet sufficient to replace echocardiography, but it establishes a first proof-of-concept that LLMs can extract latent structural signal from ECG waveforms — enough to be useful for triage where echo is unavailable.

Background & objective

More than two billion people lack reliable access to echocardiography, while a 12-lead ECG costs under a few dollars and exists in almost every clinic. If structural cardiac information is even partly recoverable from the ECG, it could power triage in places where ultrasound and sonographers simply are not available.

Prior deep-learning work established that a purpose-trained network can infer reduced ejection fraction from the digital ECG signal.¹ This study tests a different and more accessible premise: that a general-purpose, off-the-shelf LLM — with no fine-tuning and no signal access, just the ECG image — can already perform meaningful cross-modal inference through prompting alone.

Methods

We assembled 311 consecutive paired ECG–echocardiogram cases from a single cardiac centre. Each 12-lead ECG image was given to three leading LLMs — Claude Opus 4, Grok 4 and GPT-4o — with a structured prompt asking for a complete echocardiographic report, in a zero-shot setting (no fine-tuning, no example reports, no signal data). Each model's output was then scored against the cardiologist-authored echo report.

Agreement was assessed with Pearson correlation and intraclass correlation for ejection fraction, Bland–Altman analysis for systematic bias, weighted Cohen's κ for ordinal grades (diastolic dysfunction, regurgitation severity), and a Friedman test with post-hoc comparisons to rank the three models.

Results

Claude Opus 4 showed the strongest concordance with cardiologists across the primary metrics, significantly ahead of the other two models (Friedman χ² = 36.3, p < 0.001).

Bland–Altman analysis for the best model showed a mean bias of just +0.3% for EF, indicating little systematic over- or under-estimation. For wall-motion abnormalities, all three models were conservative — high specificity (82.8–91.9%) but limited sensitivity (16.7–46.1%) — i.e. they avoided false alarms at the cost of missing cases. A striking secondary finding: the LLMs were more complete than the human reports, documenting ejection fraction in 99% of cases versus 87.5% for cardiologists.

Current performance is not sufficient to replace echocardiography. The value is as a triage signal, not a diagnosis.

Clinical implications & key innovation

The realistic use case is referral triage. In a setting with no echo, an LLM-generated preliminary assessment from a cheap ECG could help a health worker decide who to send onward — catching a meaningful share of significant disease at high specificity, at the cost of an ECG (a couple of dollars) rather than an echo plus transport (often 50× more).

The key innovation is the demonstration itself: that a general LLM can perform cross-modal cardiac inference — generating echo-style assessments from ECG waveforms alone, with no fine-tuning, training data, or imaging input — which implies the ECG image encodes exploitable latent structural information accessible to multimodal models.

Limitations & future work

  • Single-centre, retrospective design; external multi-centre validation across diverse pathologies is the priority.
  • Wall-motion sensitivity is low — the clearest target for improvement, e.g. via fine-tuning on annotated ECG–echo pairs.
  • Zero-shot LLM outputs vary with prompt and model version and are not calibrated.
  • Next directions: multimodal inputs (clinical history + ECG), LLM ensembles, uncertainty/confidence scoring, feature-level explainability, and a prospective trial measuring time-to-referral and patient outcomes.
